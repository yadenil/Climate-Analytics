## 4. Chosen Time Management Strategy

### Selected strategy: Eisenhower Matrix with time blocking

I used the Eisenhower Matrix to sort the tasks into urgent/important groups, then translated that prioritization into a time-blocked calendar. This strategy was effective because the exercise presented a fixed working window (8:00 am to 5:00 pm) and multiple competing demands with deadlines.

- **Urgent and important:** Client issue, marketing deliverable, management report, vendor call, stakeholder presentation.
- **Important but less urgent:** QA coordination, onboarding, intern training, remote team check-in.
- **Less urgent:** Lunch and general support emails.

I also applied time blocking by assigning concrete start and end times for each task, which made the schedule more realistic and reduced the risk of spending too much time on one item.

## 5. Reflection on Time Management

This exercise reinforced that effective time management requires both prioritization and realistic scheduling. I started by identifying the tasks that were time-critical and mission-critical, then built a schedule around them.

### What worked well
- **Prioritization first:** Using the Eisenhower Matrix helped me avoid the trap of spending the morning on low-value tasks.
- **Fixed deadlines anchored the schedule:** The vendor call at 1:00 pm and the stakeholder presentation at 2:00 pm provided important anchor points around which the day was structured.
- **Buffer time:** Including a 30-minute buffer in the late afternoon allowed for spillover and reduced the stress of rigid timing.

### What I learned
- **Not everything needs to be done today:** Deferring the webinar, planning meeting, feedback analysis, and conference planning was a conscious, strategic choice.
- **Team-facing tasks still need time:** Onboarding and training were medium priority because they unblock colleagues, even if they are not immediately urgent.
- **Breaks matter:** Scheduling lunch was both a personal well-being decision and a commitment to maintaining productivity for the second half of the day.

### Improvement opportunities
- I could improve time estimation by tracking how long each task actually takes, especially for collaborative activities like meetings and onboarding.
- In future planning, I would reserve a slightly larger chunk of time for unplanned urgent requests, since they often arise in a fintech workday.

### Final insight
Managing this exercise showed that strong time management is a combination of strategic choice and disciplined execution. By choosing the right 11 tasks and scheduling them clearly, I was able to balance customer needs, team support, and project deadlines within the available workday.
